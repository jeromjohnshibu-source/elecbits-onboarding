01 — What Is an Electronic Product

Everything around you is
an electronic product.

An electronic product is any device that controls the flow of electricity through circuits to perform a useful function. The phone in your pocket, the inverter in your home, the sensor in your car's brakes — all of them are electronic products. They combine three fundamental layers that work together to bring a product to life.

REAL EXAMPLE: SMARTPHONE
Hardware → Snapdragon chip, battery, 200MP camera sensor, GPS
Firmware → Android bootloader, camera ISP drivers, touch controller
Enclosure → Aluminium frame, Gorilla Glass front and back
A real PCB — the hardware layer of a mini Android PC
Why all three layers matter
Great hardware with bad firmware = a broken product. Great firmware with a cheap enclosure = a product that fails in the field. All three layers must be designed and validated together — that's the art of electronic product development.

06 — Key Terms to Know

The language of the
electronics industry.

These are the terms you'll hear in every meeting, every design review, and every project at Elecbits. Understanding them on Day 1 will give you a significant head start.

PCB — Printed Circuit Board
The green (or sometimes blue/black) board that physically holds and electrically connects all components. Every electronic product has one. It's like the skeleton and nervous system of the device — components are soldered onto it, and copper traces carry electrical signals between them.
BOM — Bill of Materials
A complete, structured list of every single component used in a product — including part numbers, quantities, manufacturers, and cost. Think of it as a recipe with exact ingredients and quantities. A wrong BOM = wrong parts ordered = project delayed. Getting the BOM right early is critical.
SMT — Surface Mount Technology
The most common method for assembling PCBs. Components are placed directly onto the surface of the board (not inserted through holes) using solder paste and a reflow oven. SMT enables miniaturization — it's why your phone is thin. An SMT line can place thousands of components per hour.
DFM — Design for Manufacturability
A design review process where engineers check whether a product can actually be manufactured efficiently, reliably, and at cost. A beautiful design that's impossible to assemble at scale is useless. DFM catches these issues early — before expensive tooling or production runs begin.
ODM — Original Design Manufacturer
A company that designs a product from scratch and then manufactures it for another brand to sell. The brand gets their logo on it, but ODM owns the design. Elecbits operates as an ODM — we design products for clients who may sell them under their own brand.
EMS — Electronics Manufacturing Services
A company that manufactures products designed by other companies. If you give an EMS company your Gerber files and BOM, they build it. Unlike ODM, EMS doesn't design — they execute. Foxconn (Apple's manufacturer) is the world's largest EMS company.
ESDM — Electronics System Design & Manufacturing
The full spectrum of the electronics value chain — from circuit design and firmware development all the way through manufacturing, testing, and delivery. ESDM is the official government classification India uses for its $300B target sector. Elecbits is an ESDM company.
Gerber Files
The standard file format that describes a PCB layout — every copper trace, drill hole, silkscreen label, and solder mask is encoded in these files. When a PCB design is complete, Gerbers are sent to a fabrication house to get the bare boards made. Named after the original creator, Gerber Scientific.
MOQ — Minimum Order Quantity
The smallest number of units a supplier will sell in one order. If an IC has an MOQ of 100, you can't order just 5 pieces — you buy 100 or nothing. MOQs exist because suppliers have fixed setup costs. At Elecbits, negotiating good MOQs is critical to keeping project costs down.
TAT — Turnaround Time
How long it takes to complete a process or deliver a result. PCB fabrication TAT = 3 days means bare boards arrive in 3 days. Quote TAT = 24 hours means a price is given within 24 hours of receiving enquiry. At Elecbits, reducing TAT at every step is a core competitive advantage.

01 — Setting the Context

Every product travels through
the same value chain.

Before you understand the three models, you need to understand the electronics value chain — the journey a product takes from idea to end customer. At each step, a different type of company takes responsibility. EMS, ODM, and OEM describe which steps a company owns.

The key question for every business
At each stage of this chain, someone must own it. Does your company own the design? Does it own the manufacturing? Does it own the brand? The answer determines your business model — and your margins.
02 — EMS: Electronics Manufacturing Services

Pure manufacturing.
No design, no brand.

An EMS company manufactures electronic products on behalf of other companies. The client brings the design — the complete specifications, PCB files, BOM — and the EMS company's job is to build it, test it, and deliver it. Accurately. Repeatedly. At scale.

What an EMS company does
1
Receives design files (Gerbers, BOM, firmware) from the OEM client
2
Sources and procures all components from the BOM
3
Assembles PCBs using SMT and box build processes
4
Tests every unit for functionality, quality, and compliance
5
Delivers finished goods — client's logo on the box
What EMS Does Not Own
The design, the IP, the brand name. The product leaves the factory with someone else's logo. If the client takes their files elsewhere, the EMS company loses the business.
Foxconn — world's largest EMS company, assembles Apple iPhones
Real World Example
Foxconn is the world's largest EMS company. They build Apple iPhones, Nintendo Switch consoles, HP laptops — but none of those are "Foxconn products." Their factories do the work. Apple owns the design and the brand. Foxconn gets paid per unit assembled.
03 — ODM: Original Design Manufacturing

Design it. Build it.
Let someone else brand it.

An ODM company both designs and manufactures products. The client doesn't bring a design — the ODM already has a product (or builds one from scratch for the client). The client simply puts their logo on it and sells it. The ODM owns the intellectual property — the design, the circuit, the firmware architecture.

Two types of ODM relationships
White Label ODM
ODM has an existing product design. Multiple brands can licence and rebrand it. Same hardware, different logos. Common in chargers, routers, power banks.
Custom ODM
ODM designs a product from scratch specifically for one client. Client may get exclusive rights. Higher cost, but the product is unique. This is Elecbits' primary model.
WHY CLIENTS CHOOSE ODM
Faster time to market — they don't need an in-house engineering team. The ODM has the expertise, the tools, and the manufacturing line. A startup can go from idea to product in weeks instead of years.
Pegatron — a major ODM/EMS partner for Apple and other brands
ELECBITS EXAMPLE
A startup comes to Elecbits wanting a smart IoT motor driver. Elecbits designs the hardware, writes the firmware, manufactures the boards, and tests them. The startup gets finished units with their brand. Elecbits owns the design IP unless otherwise agreed.
KEY RISK FOR CLIENT
In a white-label ODM deal, your competitor can get the same product. IP exclusivity agreements are critical if you want a truly differentiated product.
04 — OEM: Original Equipment Manufacturer

Own the design. Own the brand.
Outsource the making.

Despite the name, an OEM today typically means a brand company that owns the product design and IP but outsources manufacturing to an EMS or ODM partner. They focus on what creates the most value — innovation, branding, and distribution — and let specialists handle the factory floor.

What an OEM focuses on
🔬
R&D and Product Innovation
Designing the next generation product, owning patents
🏷️
Brand Building
Marketing, retail relationships, customer perception
📦
Distribution & Sales
Getting the product to market, managing channels
🛡️
IP Protection
Patents, trade secrets, design rights — the crown jewels
Apple — the world's most valuable OEM. Designs in Cupertino, manufactures in Asia.
THE CLASSIC EXAMPLE
Apple designs every component of the iPhone in California. They own every patent. But they don't operate a single factory — Foxconn (EMS) and Pegatron (ODM/EMS) do all the manufacturing. Apple captures 40%+ margins. Foxconn earns 2–4%.
WHY HIGH MARGINS?
OEMs earn the highest margins because they carry the most risk — they invest in R&D, own the IP, and bet on market success. If the product fails, only they lose.
05 — Side-by-Side Comparison

Three models. One table.
See it clearly.

EMS Sweet Spot
High-volume, repeatable manufacturing for established designs. Best when you need scale and cost efficiency.
ODM Sweet Spot
Startups or brands that need a product fast without an in-house engineering team. Speed and expertise over IP control.
OEM Sweet Spot
Established companies with strong engineering teams that want full IP control, brand ownership, and long-term differentiation.

They don't amplify. They shape.

Passive components can't introduce energy into a circuit — they only store, dissipate, or redirect it. Think of them as the traffic controllers of a circuit. No power required to operate them.

🔴
Resistor
Limits and controls the flow of electrical current. Like a narrowing in a pipe — the narrower it is, the less water flows. Measured in Ohms (Ω). Used everywhere to set voltage levels, protect LEDs, and create reference signals.
🔵
Capacitor
Stores electrical charge and releases it quickly. Like a tiny rechargeable battery — it charges up and discharges when needed. Measured in Farads (F). Used for filtering noise, stabilizing voltage, and timing circuits.
🟡
Inductor
Stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through it. Resists sudden changes in current. Used in power supply filters, RF circuits, and motor drivers. Measured in Henrys (H).
Why passives matter at Elecbits
Every PCB Elecbits designs has hundreds of passive components. A wrong resistor value can cause a device to overheat. A wrong capacitor can cause oscillation failures. Getting the passive selection right in the BOM is a core engineering skill.
02 — Active Components

They amplify, process, and control.

Active components need external power to operate — and they can amplify signals, switch circuits, and process data. They are the intelligent core of every electronic product.

🧠
Integrated Circuit (IC)
A miniaturised electronic circuit packed onto a tiny silicon chip. An IC can contain billions of transistors. It does the heavy lifting — amplifying, switching, processing. Your phone's camera processor, WiFi chip, and audio codec are all ICs.
⚙️
Microcontroller (MCU)
A complete computer on a single chip — it has a processor, memory (RAM + Flash), and input/output pins all built in. Used to run the logic of a product. The brains of an IoT sensor, a motor driver, or a smart light switch.
📡
Sensor
Converts a physical quantity (temperature, pressure, light, motion) into an electrical signal that a microcontroller can read. A temperature sensor outputs a voltage proportional to heat. Sensors are the product's connection to the real world.

⚙️ Microcontroller (MCU)
Everything in one chip — CPU, RAM, Flash memory, GPIO pins, ADC, timers, communication interfaces (UART, SPI, I2C). Plug in power and it runs. Designed for embedded, real-time control tasks.

01 — What is a PCB?

More than just a green board.

A Printed Circuit Board (PCB) is a flat board made of insulating material (usually fibreglass) with thin copper tracks printed/etched onto it. These copper tracks are the wires — they connect all the components soldered onto the board. Without a PCB, you'd need metres of loose wire to connect every component, which would be unreliable and impossibly complex.

The PCB does two jobs simultaneously
1. Mechanical support — It physically holds all components in place.
2. Electrical connections — The copper traces carry signals and power between components. No external wiring needed.
A real PCB — components soldered on, copper traces visible between pads
02 — Types of PCBs

Layers determine complexity.

Not all PCBs are equal. The number of layers determines how many signals can be routed and how complex the design can be. More layers = more routing space = more complex products = higher cost.

1️⃣
Single-Layer PCB
Copper traces on one side only. Simplest and cheapest. Used for simple products — basic LED drivers, simple power supplies, low-complexity consumer gadgets. Limited in routing complexity.
2️⃣
Double-Layer PCB
Copper traces on both sides, connected by drilled holes called vias. Much more routing space. Used for most mid-complexity products — motor controllers, IoT devices, industrial sensors. Elecbits most common format.
🔢
Multi-Layer PCB
4, 6, 8+ layers sandwiched together. Used for high-speed, high-density designs — smartphones, laptops, servers, ADAS ECUs. Dedicated layers for power planes and ground planes reduce noise. More expensive and complex to fabricate.
At Elecbits, we work with all three
Simple Box Build projects might use 1–2 layer boards. Complex ODM products — motor drivers with high-speed communication — can require 4-layer boards with controlled impedance traces.
03 — The PCB Design-to-Fab Process

How a PCB goes from idea to physical board.

A PCB doesn't just appear — it goes through a structured design and fabrication process before a single component is placed on it.

1
Schematic Design
Engineer draws the circuit on paper/software — defines which components connect to which. No physical dimensions yet.
2
PCB Layout
Engineer places components and routes copper traces on the physical board. Tool: KiCad or Altium. Output: Gerber files.
3
DFM Review
Design for Manufacturability check — will this board be easy to assemble at scale? Any issues with component spacing or via sizes?
4
Fabrication
Gerbers sent to fab house. Board is etched, drilled, plated, and cut. Bare boards arrive in 3–5 days for standard, 24h for express.
5
Assembly
Components are placed and soldered onto the bare board using SMT or THT processes. The board becomes functional.

01 — What is Hardware?

The physical world.

Hardware is everything you can touch — the PCB, the chips, the resistors, the connectors, the enclosure. It is the physical manifestation of an electronic product. Hardware sets the limits: how fast the processor can run, how much memory is available, how many sensors are connected.

Hardware is permanent
Once a PCB is manufactured, its hardware capabilities are fixed. You can't add more GPIO pins by updating software. This is why hardware design decisions made early in a project have long-term consequences — and why DFM reviews and early prototyping at Elecbits are so important.
02 — What is Firmware?

Software that lives inside the hardware.

Firmware is software that is permanently programmed into a hardware device. Unlike apps on your phone, firmware runs directly on the hardware — there's no operating system in the middle (usually). It controls every behaviour of the device: when to turn on an LED, how to read a sensor, how to communicate over WiFi.

What firmware controls
⚙️ How sensors are read and interpreted
📡 Communication protocols (WiFi, BLE, UART)
💡 When and how actuators are triggered
🔋 Power management and sleep modes
🛡️ Safety and error handling logic
Key firmware properties
🔧 Written in C or C++ for most embedded systems
⚡ Runs in microseconds — real-time execution
💾 Stored in Flash memory on the microcontroller
🔄 Can be updated (OTA updates in IoT products)
🔒 Often the difference between a good and bad product

01 — Technical Terms

The physical building blocks.

BOM — Bill of Materials
A structured spreadsheet listing every single component in a product — part number, quantity, manufacturer, supplier, price. It's the master document from which components are ordered. An incorrect BOM is one of the most expensive mistakes in electronics manufacturing.
Gerbers — Gerber Files
Standard file format that describes a PCB layout. Engineers export Gerbers from their design tool (KiCad/Altium) and send them to the fab house. Contains all information needed to physically create the board — copper layers, drill holes, solder mask, silkscreen.
Proto — Prototyping
Building the first physical version of a product to test the design. Not a finished product — it's a learning tool. Prototypes are expected to have issues. At Elecbits, a fast, cheap prototype is better than a slow, expensive one — speed of learning matters.
PCBA — Assembly
Printed Circuit Board Assembly — the process of placing and soldering components onto a bare PCB. A 'bare board' becomes a 'PCBA' after assembly. This is what Elecbits' SMT line produces.
Test/QC — Testing
Verifying that an assembled board meets its specifications — functional test, parametric test, environmental test. Testing is a mandatory step before any board ships from Elecbits.
Yield % — Yield
The percentage of boards that pass testing on the first attempt without rework. A 95% yield means 95 out of 100 boards pass first time. Higher yield = lower cost, faster delivery. Improving yield is a continuous focus in manufacturing.
Lead Time — Lead Time
How long it takes from placing an order to receiving the goods. PCB fab lead time might be 3–5 days. Component lead times can be 3–52 weeks depending on availability. Long component lead times are a major project risk.
02 — Workflow Terms

How projects and orders flow.

RFQ — Request for Quotation
A formal request sent to suppliers or manufacturers asking for a price. Elecbits sends RFQs to component suppliers when building a BOM cost estimate. Clients send RFQs to Elecbits when they want pricing for a project.
MOQ — Minimum Order Quantity
The minimum number of units a supplier will sell. If a capacitor has an MOQ of 1000, you can't buy just 10. MOQs affect project costs significantly — especially for prototypes. A key procurement negotiation lever.
Vendor — Vendor
A supplier of components, materials, or services. Elecbits maintains a vetted vendor list for components, PCB fab, and specialised assembly. Vendor quality directly affects product quality.
TAT — Turnaround Time
How quickly a process is completed from start to finish. PCB fab TAT: 3 days. Quote TAT: 24 hours. Faster TAT is a core competitive advantage for Elecbits. XoR tracks TAT for every step.
03 — Engineering Terms

What engineers say all day.

Schematic — Schematic
A diagram showing how all components in a circuit are electrically connected. Like a blueprint for a circuit — no physical dimensions, just connections. The first step in PCB design. Created in KiCad, Altium, or Eagle.
PCB Layout — PCB Layout
The physical design of a PCB — where each component sits on the board and how copper traces route between them. Converted to Gerber files for fabrication. A good layout minimises noise, heat, and assembly difficulty.
Bring-up — Bring-Up
The process of powering up a newly assembled PCB for the first time and verifying basic functionality. Are power rails at the right voltage? Does the microcontroller boot? Does firmware communicate? Bring-up is always an exciting (and sometimes smoky) moment.
Debug — Debugging
The process of finding and fixing problems — in hardware (wrong component value, trace short) or firmware (logic error, timing issue). Debugging is a core daily activity for engineers at Elecbits. Tools: oscilloscope, logic analyser, JTAG debugger.
V1, V2, V3... — Revision
Each iteration of a hardware design gets a version number. V1 is the first prototype, V2 incorporates fixes from V1 testing, and so on. All revisions are tracked in XoR. Never lose track of which version a board is — it matters enormously.

Get a formal handover

Shadow before you execute
In your first week, shadow the lead on your first task before taking ownership. Ask your PM for a 15-minute project context walkthrough.

At end of each day, update your task status. Even 'in progress, no blockers' is enough. This keeps your PM informed without them having to chase you.
